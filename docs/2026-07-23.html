The first reading — Jeremiah 2:1-3, 7-8, 12-13

Jeremiah opens with tenderness before he turns. God recalls the honeymoon period: Israel following through the desert, devoted as a young bride, sacred as first fruits. The memory is not sentimental — it is the ground from which the accusation grows. You were that, and look at what you became.

The charge lands in two specific failures. The priests and prophets stopped asking where God was; they turned to Baal and went after what Jeremiah calls useless idols, empty things. And then the image that closes the passage: Israel has abandoned the source of living water and dug its own cisterns — cracked ones that cannot even hold what they catch. The image is deliberately absurd. You had a spring and you chose a broken clay pit. The heavens, God says, should shudder at this. It is not primarily a moral failure but a failure of basic sense.

The psalm answers Jeremiah almost directly. Where Jeremiah's God mourns a people who left the fountain, Psalm 36 celebrates the ones who stayed: with you is the fountain of life, from your delightful stream you give them to drink. The psalm does not pretend the forsaking never happened. It simply holds out what remains available.

The Gospel — Matthew 13:10-17

The disciples want to know why Jesus uses parables with the crowds rather than speaking plainly. His answer is harder than it sounds. It is not that the crowds are stupid or that Jesus is withholding out of cruelty. He is describing a condition: people who look without seeing, hear without understanding, whose hearts have grown so thick that perception itself has become difficult. He quotes Isaiah to show this is not new — the prophets recognized the same closing-off in their own day.

What the disciples have is not superior intelligence. They have proximity and willingness. They stayed close enough to ask the question. Many prophets and righteous people, Jesus says, longed for exactly what the disciples are casually receiving and never got it.

Where the two readings meet

These two passages are on separate tracks — Jeremiah runs on his own weekday cycle, independent of Matthew — but they turn out to be asking the same question from different angles. Jeremiah's people had access to living water and chose broken cisterns instead. The crowds in Matthew have a teacher standing in front of them and cannot quite receive what he is offering. Both texts are less about dramatic apostasy than about a slow narrowing of perception: the gradual preference for smaller, more manageable substitutes until the capacity for the real thing has quietly atrophied.

The uncomfortable detail in Matthew is that the disciples' seeing is treated as a gift, not an achievement. They did not earn clearer eyes. They were given them — which raises the obvious question about what any of us have done with what we've been given to see.

To carry today

What smaller, more manageable thing have you chosen to want, rather than the thing you actually need?
