A people who left a spring to dig holes in the ground — and are still digging.

The first reading — Jeremiah 2:1-3, 7-8, 12-13

Jeremiah is standing at the beginning of his ministry, sometime in the late seventh century, when the religious life of Jerusalem has been running on fumes for generations. God tells him to go and recite a kind of wedding memory — the early days in the desert, when Israel followed like a bride, before there was a land or a harvest or a temple. The tenderness of those opening lines makes what follows more cutting, not less. When God brought the people into the land they had been promised, the first thing they did was defile it.

The indictment moves through the whole chain of leadership: priests who never asked where God was, prophets who preached in the name of Baal, shepherds who simply went their own way. Then comes the image that holds everything together. God names two failures, and the second is stranger than the first. Forsaking the source of living water is bad enough. But the people didn't simply walk away into emptiness — they worked hard in the other direction, digging their own cisterns, cracked ones that can't hold water. The sin is not laziness. It is the considerable effort of constructing a substitute that cannot do what it promises.

The Gospel — Matthew 13:10-17

The disciples pull Jesus aside after a parable and ask why he speaks that way to the crowds. His answer is more unsettling than explanatory. He quotes Isaiah's description of a people who are hearing without understanding, looking without seeing — and then says that the disciples are not that. Their eyes and ears are open. What makes the difference is not spelled out. But the beatitude at the end is real: many righteous people strained to perceive what these fishermen and tax collectors are perceiving without effort simply by being present.

What the two texts share

These readings are on separate tracks in the lectionary — Jeremiah runs continuously through weeks of weekdays, while the Gospel follows its own path — so any connection is oblique rather than designed. Still, the same basic question runs through both: what makes perception possible, and what shuts it down? Jeremiah's people had the prophets, the law, the memory of the exodus. The crowds in Matthew had Jesus standing in front of them. In both cases, access to the truth was not the problem. The cisterns in Jeremiah are a useful image here — they suggest that the obstacle to perceiving God is rarely a shortage of information, but rather a prior investment in something else, something we have already labored to build and are reluctant to abandon.

The psalm pulls both readings toward their resolution: the fountain of life is there. The question is whether a person is still standing at the broken cistern, convinced it will eventually fill.

To carry today

What have you worked hardest to build that is quietly holding nothing?
