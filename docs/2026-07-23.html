Sixteenth Week in Ordinary Time

God remembers the love of the beginning, and keeps offering the living water we keep refusing.

First readingJeremiah 2:1-3, 7-8, 12-13
PsalmPsalm 36:6-7, 8-9, 10-11
GospelMatthew 13:10-17

There is a marriage gone wrong at the heart of today's first reading, and a crowd that cannot quite see what is standing right in front of them in the Gospel. Both are stories about a strange and persistent blindness — the kind that is not accidental but chosen, not a failure of information but a failure of desire. What makes these readings worth sitting with is that neither God nor Jesus gives up because of it.

The first reading — Jeremiah 2:1-3, 7-8, 12-13

Jeremiah is one of the great tragic figures of the Hebrew scriptures. He prophesied for decades in Jerusalem before its fall to Babylon in 587 B.C., watching the city ignore warning after warning. What we have today is almost the opening of his public ministry — a kind of prosecution speech delivered on God's behalf to the city. It is worth knowing that the speech is not angry in the way we might expect. It opens with something closer to grief.

God recalls the early days of Israel's relationship with him — the desert years, the wilderness wandering after the Exodus. That period was not, historically, a golden age in any simple sense; the wilderness stories are full of complaint and rebellion. But the image here reaches past the historical record toward something true in another register: there was a time of nearness, of dependence, of being carried. "I remember the devotion of your youth," God says, "how you loved me as a bride." The marriage metaphor would have been startling when Jeremiah first spoke it, and it remains startling. The God of the universe is describing himself as a husband who remembers when his wife first loved him.

Then the indictment. God brought Israel into "the garden land," the promised territory, and what happened next was a betrayal on every level of society: priests who stopped asking where God was, scribes and lawyers who knew the law but had forgotten its author, shepherds — this is the word for leaders, kings and elders — who outright rebelled, and prophets who were prophesying in the name of Baal, the Canaanite storm-deity whose worship was everywhere in the culture around them. Every layer of leadership had failed.

The image that closes the passage is one of the most arresting in all of Jeremiah. God calls on the heavens themselves to be horrified at what has happened, and then delivers the double charge: Israel has abandoned "the source of living waters" and instead dug cisterns — holding tanks carved out of limestone — that are broken and cannot hold water. It is almost darkly comic if you think about it too long. Cisterns were genuinely useful things, but they held only rainwater, stagnant and still, and this image is of ones that are cracked besides. Someone has walked away from a running spring to lug water in a leaking bucket. The sheer foolishness of it is part of the point.

Psalm 36 answers this reading with precise and beautiful irony. "With you is the fountain of life, O Lord" — the very thing Jeremiah accuses Israel of abandoning. The psalm does not argue or explain; it simply sets the reality back in front of us: the mercy reaches to heaven, the stream is delightful, the light is yours when you stand in God's light.

The Gospel — Matthew 13:10-17

Matthew 13 is the great parable chapter, and what we get today is not a parable itself but a conversation about why Jesus uses them. The disciples ask a reasonable question: why not just say plainly what you mean? The crowds are right there.

Jesus's answer has troubled readers for a long time, because it sounds almost cruel at first hearing — as if he is deliberately obscuring the truth from people so they cannot be converted. But the logic is more precise than that. He is quoting Isaiah 6, the prophet's own call narrative, in which Isaiah is sent to a people whose hearts have already closed themselves. Jesus is observing the same pattern, not engineering it. The parables are not a mechanism of exclusion; they are a form of truth-telling that can only be received by those who are willing to receive it. A story about seeds and soil lands differently depending on what kind of soil you are.

The word for "gross" in the Isaiah quotation — "gross is the heart of this people" — carries the sense of grown thick, calloused, like skin that has been rubbed so long it no longer feels. It is not a permanent condition; conversion is still explicitly named as what would happen if they would only see and hear. The door is not locked from the outside.

The blindness that is a choice

What Jeremiah and Matthew share is the portrait of a specifically chosen blindness. Israel in Jeremiah does not lack information; she has the law, the prophets, the priests, the memory of the Exodus. The crowds around Jesus do not lack access; they are standing in front of him. What is missing in both cases is the willingness to let what they see actually land, to let it cost them the comfortable explanations they have already arranged. That is the harder diagnosis, because it applies well past ancient Israel and first-century Galilee.

The good news in both readings is the same: the living water is still flowing, the door is still open, and the disciples — ordinary, confused, often stumbling — are told that their eyes are blessed because they see.

To carry today

Consider where in your life you might already have your explanation settled before you look. The broken cisterns in Jeremiah are not wicked things — they are just our own anxious arrangements, the strategies we trust because we made them ourselves. Ask today for the grace to look at one situation in your life with a little less certainty about what you will find, and a little more willingness to be surprised by what is actually there.
