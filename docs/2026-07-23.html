The First Reading: A Love Story Gone Wrong

We are at the very opening of Jeremiah's public ministry, sometime in the late seventh century BC. The prophet has just received his call, and God immediately sends him to deliver a heartbreaking word to Jerusalem. What makes this passage so striking is its tone: God does not open with a legal indictment but with a love poem. He recalls the tenderness of Israel's early days — the wilderness years after the Exodus — as a honeymoon, a time when the people followed him faithfully through a barren land, trusting entirely in his provision.

That memory makes what comes next all the more devastating. When God brought Israel into the good land — flowing with fruit and abundance — they took it and desecrated it. Priests, legal experts, shepherds, prophets: every layer of leadership had turned away. And God's verdict lands in one of Scripture's most vivid images. His people have committed two intertwined sins: they have abandoned him, the *source of living waters*, and they have replaced him with cracked, broken cisterns — containers they dug themselves that cannot hold a drop. The image is almost comic in its tragedy: leaving a fresh, flowing spring to go haul water from a leaking hole in the ground.

The Gospel: Eyes That See, Ears That Hear

In Matthew 13, Jesus is mid-parable — he has just told the parable of the sower — and his disciples pull him aside with a sharp question: why parables? Why not just say things plainly? Jesus' answer is both a pastoral explanation and a quiet indictment. He quotes Isaiah 6, the passage in which the prophet is warned that the people will hear his words and not take them in, look at his signs and not register them. Their hearts have grown thick and slow. This is not God arbitrarily withholding understanding; it is the tragic logic of repeated refusal. When a person consistently chooses not to receive, the capacity to receive gradually closes over.

But then Jesus turns to his disciples and his voice warms: *blessed are your eyes, because they see.* Many prophets and righteous people longed for this moment and never reached it. The disciples are living inside the answer to centuries of longing.

Where the Readings Meet

Today the lectionary has given us a genuinely strong pairing, and the connecting thread is the tragedy of chosen blindness. In Jeremiah, Israel had the living God speaking directly to them, leading them, feeding them — and they stopped seeing it. They looked at the great spring and chose their own crumbling cisterns instead. In the Gospel, Jesus describes the same movement in new terms: people who look without seeing, hear without understanding, because something in them has hardened against the light.

The Responsorial Psalm sits perfectly between them. It sings of what the people in both readings are turning away from: God's mercy reaching to the clouds, a delightful stream, a fountain of life. The psalm is the treasure; Jeremiah and Matthew both describe people who have talked themselves out of wanting it.

And yet neither passage ends in pure condemnation. Jeremiah's lament is still a lover's cry — God would not grieve this way if the door were fully closed. And Jesus' words to the disciples are pure gift: *your eyes are blessed.* The call embedded in both texts is the same: do not let your heart grow thick. Stay near the spring.
