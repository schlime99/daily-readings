Saint Mary Magdalene

Feast

Searching the dark city for a beloved who turns out to be already standing behind you.

First readingSong of Songs 3:1-4b
PsalmPsalm 63:2-9
GospelJohn 20:1-2, 11-18

The first reading — Song of Songs 3:1-4b

The Song of Songs is a strange book to find in the Bible. It is a love poem — erotic, restless, not obviously religious at all. The bride wakes in the night and her beloved is gone. She goes out into the streets looking for him. She accosts the watchmen. She searches. And then, almost anticlimactically, almost before the reader can register it: she finds him. The text doesn't explain what happened. There's no reunion speech. One moment she is asking strangers if they've seen the one her heart loves, and the next he is there.

The tradition has long read this poem as something more than a human love story — the soul searching for God, Israel longing for the Lord, the Church awaiting Christ. That reading is not wrong, but it doesn't flatten the poem into allegory either. What it does is take seriously what the poem already knows: that a search conducted in the dark, with increasing desperation, ending in a finding that is almost inexplicable, is exactly what love is like. The bride doesn't find her beloved because she searched better. She finds him when she has nearly given up asking.

The Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18

Mary Magdalene comes to the tomb while it is still dark. That detail matters. She is not there in the morning light — she is there in the last hour of night, when the darkness hasn't yet lifted. She sees the stone rolled away and her first thought is loss compounded: they have taken him. Even an empty tomb reads, at first, as one more theft.

She stays. Peter and the beloved disciple come and go, but she stays, weeping outside. When she finally looks in, she sees angels. They ask why she is weeping. Then she turns — and here is the scene that holds the whole feast together — she turns and sees Jesus standing there, and does not know it is Jesus. She thinks he is the gardener. She is still in the mode of searching, still trying to find the body, still operating as if the problem is that someone has moved him. Tell me where you laid him and I will take him.

Then he says her name. Just that. Mary. And she turns — the text says she turned twice in the space of a few verses, which is worth sitting with — and she knows.

What he says next is harder: stop holding on to me. Something about the embrace she reaches for, or has already reached for, must end. He is not gone, but he is not in the form she is grasping for. She has to let that go before she can do the thing he sends her to do. She goes. I have seen the Lord. The tradition calls her apostle to the apostles because of those four words, spoken to people who had not stayed, who had gone home.

The search and the finding

The two texts are in genuine conversation today. Both are about looking in the dark for someone who cannot be found by looking harder. The bride searches the city. Mary searches the tomb, then the garden. In both cases, the finding happens when the searcher is not exactly looking — the bride has just left the watchmen, empty-handed, when she finds him; Mary has just turned away from the angels when she turns and sees a man she doesn't yet recognize.

There is something almost frustrating about this. It suggests that the frantic search is necessary but not sufficient — that the beloved is found, in both stories, not as a reward for searching but almost in spite of it. The Song of Songs doesn't explain this and the Gospel doesn't either. Mary is weeping when she turns. She is not triumphant or composed. She is a woman in grief, asking a stranger for a body.

The feast of Mary Magdalene doesn't soften that. She is patron of this kind of love: the kind that shows up in the dark, that stays when others leave, that mistakes the living Christ for a gardener, and that has to be called by name before it can see.

To carry today

Where are you still searching for a body — still trying to recover a form of God or faith or love that is gone — while something alive stands just behind you, waiting to say your name?
