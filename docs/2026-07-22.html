Feast of Saint Mary Magdalene

Feast of Saint Mary Magdalene

  • First Reading: Song of Songs 3:1–4b *(or 2 Corinthians 5:14–17)*
  • Responsorial Psalm: Psalm 63:2, 3–4, 5–6, 8–9 — *My soul is thirsting for you, O Lord my God.*
  • Gospel: John 20:1–2, 11–18

---

The woman and her feast

Mary Magdalene holds a singular place in the Easter story: she is the first witness of the risen Lord, the one who carried the news to the apostles. Pope Francis elevated her memorial to a full feast in 2016, and the Church sometimes calls her *apostola apostolorum* — the apostle to the apostles. Today's readings are chosen precisely for her, and the connections between them are genuine and beautiful.

---

The First Reading: a soul searching in the dark

The Song of Songs is a collection of love poetry, set in the Hebrew wisdom tradition, celebrating the intensity of human love — and, in the Church's long reading of it, the love between God and the soul, or between Christ and his people. Chapter 3 opens in the middle of the night. The Bride wakes and reaches for her beloved, but he is not there. She gets up, goes out into the city streets, asks the night watchmen if they have seen him — and then, just as she leaves them behind, she finds him. The passage ends at the moment of finding, not the reunion itself, which only heightens the urgency. What drives this woman is not duty or obligation but a love that will not let her rest: *him whom my heart loves* — the phrase recurs four times in just a few verses, like a heartbeat.

---

The Gospel: searching in a garden before dawn

John's resurrection account is set in darkness too. Mary Magdalene comes to the tomb while it is still night, discovers the stone rolled away, and runs to tell the disciples. But she comes back. The disciples come and go; Mary stays. She weeps outside the tomb, bends to look in, speaks to angels, then turns and speaks to someone she takes to be the gardener. She is not passive grief — she is actively searching: *tell me where you laid him, and I will take him.* Then comes the single word that changes everything: *Mary.* Her name, spoken by her Teacher, and she knows him instantly.

---

The thread between them: love that seeks and finds

The parallel is striking and clearly intentional. Both women are out searching in the dark, driven by love they cannot suppress. Both ask passersby for help and are not satisfied. Both find what — *who* — they are looking for just at the point where the text seems to have run out of options. The Song of Songs does not explain how the Bride finds her beloved; John does not let us inside the moment of recognition until Jesus speaks her name. In both cases, love precedes understanding. Mary does not find the risen Christ because she has the right theology; she finds him because she will not stop looking.

Psalm 63 ties both readings together from within: the soul that thirsts like parched earth, that clings fast, that lifts its hands in the dark. This is the interior portrait of what the Bride and Mary Magdalene are each living out.

The alternate First Reading from 2 Corinthians — *the love of Christ impels us* — shifts from narrative to reflection, offering Paul's theological commentary on exactly what we see dramatized in the Gospel. Mary no longer knows Christ simply as a beloved teacher; after Easter, she knows him as the risen Lord. Old things have passed away; she goes out to announce something entirely new.

---

To carry today

Where in your own life are you still standing outside an empty tomb, weeping, when the risen Christ may already be near — waiting only for you to hear your name called?
