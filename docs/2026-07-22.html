The first reading — Song of Songs 3:1-4b

The Song of Songs is the strangest book in the Bible to find at Mass. It is a love poem, erotic and restless, set in the streets of a city at night. The bride gets up from her bed and goes looking — through the streets, past the watchmen, past anyone who might help — and finds the one she loves almost by accident, almost before she can say where she found him. The poem ends in the middle of the embrace.

The Church has long read this book as something more than a wedding song: the soul in search of God, desire as a form of theology. That reading doesn't erase the poem's plainness — the woman really is looking, really is desperate, really does find — but it gives the searching a shape that fits today's feast exactly. Mary Magdalene at the tomb is the bride in the streets, except her beloved is not missing. He is present. She cannot recognize him.

The Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18

John's account of Easter morning is built around a misrecognition. Mary sees the risen Christ and thinks she is looking at the gardener. She is so fixed on finding the body she came to anoint that she cannot read what is standing in front of her. And then he says her name — one word, Mary — and everything reorganizes itself. She does not recognize the risen Christ by sight. She recognizes him by the way he says her name.

What happens next is often overlooked. He tells her not to hold on to him, and then sends her — go and tell my brothers. She came to tend a corpse and leaves as a witness to a resurrection. She is the first to carry the news of Easter, which is why she has been called, in a title the tradition takes seriously, the apostle to the apostles.

What the two readings share

The psalm holds both readings together: my soul is thirsting for you, my flesh pines for you, like the earth parched and without water. That thirst runs through the Song's bride in the streets and through Mary bent weeping into an empty tomb. Both are looking in the wrong place for the right person. The moment of finding, in both cases, comes not through their searching but through being known — recognized, named, encountered from the other side.

What the Song cannot give you, John completes: the beloved does not just appear. He speaks.
