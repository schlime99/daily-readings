The first reading — Song of Songs 3:1-4b

The woman in the Song gets up in the middle of the night because she cannot sleep without him. She goes out into the city — the streets, the crossings, the watchmen who make their rounds and cannot help her. Then, just past them, she finds him. The poem gives no explanation for why she finds him when she does. She simply does.

The Church has long read this poem as more than a love lyric, hearing in the bride's restless searching a figure for the soul that will not settle for anything less than God. That reading is old and it is earned. But it does not cancel the literal one. The woman is desperate. Her looking is physical, embodied, almost embarrassing in its urgency. She does not pray; she runs.

The Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18

Mary comes to the tomb while it is still dark. That detail is not atmospheric — she is there before sunrise because she could not wait until morning. She has come to tend a body, and the body is gone, and she weeps outside the tomb. The angels ask why she is weeping. Jesus himself asks why she is weeping. She keeps answering the same way: someone has taken him and she does not know where.

Then he says her name. One word. And she turns.

What she had been looking for was a body to anoint, a place to grieve. What she gets is her name in his mouth. The tradition has called her apostle to the apostles, and the title fits: she is the first to carry the news, commissioned by the risen Christ himself before anyone else. But the commission only comes after the recognition, and the recognition only comes through that one syllable.

What the reading opens in the Gospel

These two texts are made for each other. The Song's woman searches the city; Mary searches a garden. Both find the one they are looking for at the moment the text least explains — suddenly, after the searching has gone on long enough to be painful. In both, the finding is not the reward of technique or persistence. It just happens, the way dawn happens.

There is a difficulty John presses on, though. Jesus tells Mary to stop holding on to him. She has just found him and already she must let him go in a new way — not back into the tomb, but forward into something she cannot yet see. The feast does not smooth this over. The joy is real and the release is real and they happen at the same moment.

To carry today

Where am I still looking for a body when a name is already being spoken?
