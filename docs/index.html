Wednesday · 22 July

Saint Mary Magdalene

Feast

Searching in the dark for a beloved who cannot be found — and then, suddenly, your name in his mouth.

Song of Songs 3:1-4b
Psalm 63:2-9
John 20:1-2, 11-18

The first thing John tells us is that it was still dark.

Mary doesn't wait for dawn. She goes to the tomb in the dark, which is either the bravest thing a person can do or simply what grief does — it doesn't let you sleep, so you go. She gets there and the stone is already moved and she runs, and when she comes back the others have left and she is alone outside, weeping.

The Song of Songs has been read for centuries as the soul's search for God, and today the Church puts it directly next to this scene, and you can see why. On my bed at night I sought him whom my heart loves — I sought him but I did not find him. The bride gets up, goes into the city, asks the watchmen, keeps moving. The searching is not dignified. It has an edge of desperation to it. She will not stop until she finds him.

Mary bends and looks into the tomb and there are two angels and she tells them what she told Peter: someone has taken him and she doesn't know where. Then she turns and there is a man standing there and she thinks it is the gardener and she says — and this is the part that stops me — Sir, if you carried him away, tell me where you laid him, and I will take him. She will take him. Just tell her where he is and she will carry him herself.

Then he says her name.

Just that. Mary. One word and she knows. The tradition has long seen in this the fulfillment of everything the shepherd in John's Gospel said about sheep knowing their shepherd's voice. You can explain that theologically. But the experience it points to is simpler and harder to argue with: there are moments when you know you are known, and those moments change everything.

She calls him Rabbouni — Teacher — and he tells her not to hold on to him, because he has not yet ascended. This is a strange line and it's worth sitting with rather than explaining away. He is present but not in the old way. The relationship is being renegotiated in real time, and she is being asked to let go of what she had in order to receive what is coming. That is a genuinely difficult thing to be asked. She is being sent before she has finished receiving.

But she goes. I have seen the Lord. That is the whole message and she carries it. The tradition has called her apostle to the apostles for this reason — not as an honorific but as a plain description of what happened. She was sent. She went.

Psalm 63 sits underneath all of this like a ground note: my soul is thirsting for you, O Lord my God. Parched earth. Lifeless without water. The psalm knows that kind of longing — not the gentle, poetic kind but the dry, physical, urgent kind. And then: as with the riches of a banquet shall my soul be satisfied. The movement from thirst to feast. From dark garden to resurrection morning.

The whole feast is about what happens in that gap — the searching, the weeping, the not-finding, and then the name spoken in the dark.

To carry today

Where have you been looking in the wrong place, or the right place at the wrong time — and what would it feel like to hear your name called there?
