The First Reading: A Marriage Gone Cold

We are at the very opening of Jeremiah's public ministry, likely in the early reign of King Josiah, well before the catastrophe of Babylon. The prophet has just received his call in chapter one, and now God sends him immediately into the streets of Jerusalem with a message that is equal parts lament and accusation.

God speaks like a heartbroken spouse remembering a honeymoon that once was. There was a time — in the wilderness wandering — when Israel followed the Lord through a land with nothing to offer, sustained only by trust and love. That fidelity was beautiful. Israel was then like the first fruits of a harvest, set apart and holy.

But something shifted once they reached the Promised Land. Abundance came, and with abundance, forgetfulness. The land God gave them became the very place they defiled. Priests stopped asking where the Lord could be found. Prophets chased after Baal. Leaders turned away. And God names the double wound: they abandoned the living spring — the source — and instead dug their own cisterns. Broken ones, at that. Ones that cannot hold a drop.

The image is devastating in its practicality. A cistern in the ancient world was a hand-cut reservoir for rainwater. It was a backup, a human provision. To forsake a gushing spring for a cracked, leaking hole in the rock is not just foolish — it is a kind of madness. The heavens themselves are called to shudder at it.

The Gospel: Eyes That See, Ears That Hear

Matthew places us mid-parable-discourse. Jesus has just told the parable of the sower, and his disciples pull him aside with a practical question: why the riddles? Why not just say it plainly?

Jesus answers with something that sounds harsh on the surface but is actually a diagnosis of a spiritual condition, not a sentence. He quotes Isaiah's vision of a people who have heard so long without responding that their capacity to hear has dulled. They look but do not perceive. They listen but do not understand. Their hearts have grown thick — not because God shut them out, but because they have repeatedly chosen not to let the word land and change them.

The disciples, by contrast, are called blessed. Not because they are smarter or more deserving, but because they have remained open. They are with Jesus, asking questions, leaning in. And Jesus tells them something remarkable: prophets and righteous people across the centuries longed to witness what these fishermen and tax collectors are seeing right now, and never got to.

Where the Two Readings Meet

Today these readings are genuinely in conversation with each other, and the thread running between them is the theme of perception and its loss.

Jeremiah's Israel had access to the living water — the Lord himself — and chose the broken cistern anyway. Matthew's crowd has access to the Word made flesh standing right in front of them, and cannot see him. In both cases, the tragedy is not ignorance. It is a willful, accumulated turning away. The priests in Jeremiah's day did not forget the Lord through simple neglect — they actively chose other gods. The crowd in Matthew has hardened its heart through repeated refusal.

The Psalm bridges them perfectly. *With you is the fountain of life* — the very image Jeremiah uses for God — is sung as a confession of trust. From God's delightful stream, the faithful drink. In God's light, they see light. The Psalm is what it looks like to remain oriented toward the spring rather than digging the broken cistern; to keep one's eyes open rather than closing them against the light.

The good news in both readings is that the door is not closed. Jeremiah is still sent to cry out — there is still time. Jesus is still teaching, still explaining to those who ask. The question is whether we are among those who lean in.
