The first reading — Song of Songs 3:1-4b

The Song of Songs is a love poem that made ancient rabbis uneasy. It names no one as God. It does not mention the covenant. It is frank about longing and the body. The rabbis kept it in the canon because they read it as the people of Israel crying out for the Lord — and the Lord for them. That reading has held ever since, and it opens the poem wide.

This passage is pure night searching. The beloved rises from bed, goes out into the city, asks strangers, finds nothing. The watchmen can't help. Then, almost as an aside, almost too quickly: I had hardly left them when I found him. The finding is not dramatic. It follows the failure. It happens when she is still moving.

The Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18

Mary arrives at the tomb before the sun is up. That detail matters. She is not waiting for morning; she is out in the dark. She sees the stone moved and her first thought is theft — they have taken him. Peter and the other disciple come and go, but Mary stays and weeps.

What keeps her there is not courage. It might just be that she has nowhere else to go. She bends in, sees the angels, answers their question without flinching, turns — and does not recognize the man standing behind her. The gardener, she thinks. Sir, if you carried him away. She is still looking for a body.

Then he says her name.

That is the whole turn. Not an argument, not a sign, not a wound shown. One word. Her name, in her own language. And she knows.

Where they meet

The liturgy sets these two texts beside each other because the shape of the Song is the shape of Easter morning: night searching, failure, the sudden finding that comes without fanfare. The woman in the Song does not find her beloved because she finally searches in the right place. She finds him while she is still looking in the wrong one.

Mary Magdalene is sent — go to my brothers — before she has time to hold on to what she has found. The tradition has called her apostle to the apostles for this reason. She carries news she did not expect, about a life she does not yet fully understand.

The psalm underneath both readings is parched earth waiting for water. That image is honest about how long the searching can take.

To carry today

What are you still searching for in the dark that may already be standing right behind you?
