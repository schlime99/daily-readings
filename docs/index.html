Wednesday · 22 July

Saint Mary Magdalene

Feast

Searching the dark streets for the one you love, and finding him where you least expected.

Song of Songs 3:1-4b
Psalm 63:2-9
John 20:1-2, 11-18

The bride in the streets

The woman in the Song of Songs does not wait until morning. She rises from her bed in the middle of the night and goes out into the city looking for him. She stops strangers — the watchmen, the city guards — and asks them if they have seen him. It is not a dignified scene. It is the kind of searching that does not care how it looks.

The Song of Songs is a love poem, earthy and unguarded, and the Church has always read it on two levels at once. On the surface, two human lovers. But underneath — or maybe not underneath at all, maybe right on the surface — the soul and God. The longing is the same kind of longing. The sleeplessness is the same sleeplessness. What makes the poem so powerful is that it does not sanitize the desire into something abstract. The bride's love is embodied, urgent, almost frantic. And in that very urgency, the tradition recognized something true about the spiritual life: that we search for God the way a person searches for someone they cannot imagine living without.

The detail the lectionary cuts on is worth sitting with. She finds him almost immediately after leaving the watchmen — I had hardly left them when I found him whom my heart loves. She had been asking the wrong people. The moment she moves on, there he is. The searching was not wasted, but the finding was not where she expected.

The garden before dawn

Mary Magdalene comes to the tomb while it is still dark. John is careful about that detail. She is not there at a convenient hour. She comes in the dark, she finds the stone moved, she runs to tell the others, and then — after Peter and the beloved disciple come and go — she stays. Everyone else leaves. She stays outside weeping.

The angels ask her why she is weeping. Jesus himself, whom she does not yet recognize, asks her why she is weeping. She keeps answering the same way: they have taken my Lord and I do not know where they have put him. She is not asking about a resurrection. She is asking about a body. She wants to find him, to tend to him, to know where he is. The love driving her is not yet theological. It is just love.

And then he says her name. One word. Mary. And she turns.

That is the whole hinge of the story. Not an argument, not a sign, not an explanation. Her name, spoken by the one who knows her. The recognition comes through being known, not through seeing.

What he says next is harder: stop holding on to me. There is grief in that line. She has just found him and he is already asking her to release him. The embrace she reaches for is real — he does not tell her she is wrong to want it — but something has changed, and the new thing cannot be grasped the same way the old thing was. He is sending her somewhere. Go and tell my brothers. The woman who came looking becomes the one who is sent.

Where the two texts meet

The pairing of the Song of Songs with this Gospel is not accidental and not forced. The bride searching the city at night and Mary searching the garden before dawn are doing the same thing. Both of them are looking for someone they love in a place where he is not — and finding him somewhere just past where they stopped looking.

The tradition long ago named Mary Magdalene apostola apostolorum — the apostle to the apostles. She is the first to see the risen Lord and the first to be sent to announce it. That title was not a consolation prize. It was an acknowledgment that the searching mattered, that staying when everyone else left mattered, and that love which will not go home empty is the kind of love resurrection finds.

Psalm 63 holds all of it together: my soul is thirsting for you. Not satisfied, not arrived — thirsting. Even after the finding, the longing does not disappear. It changes shape.

To carry today

Where have you been searching in the wrong place — and what would it mean to move on and look just a little further? What is the name only God calls you by?
