Saint Mary Magdalene

Feast

Searching in the dark for a beloved who is already standing right there.

First readingSong of Songs 3:1-4b
PsalmPsalm 63:2-9
GospelJohn 20:1-2, 11-18

The first reading — Song of Songs 3:1-4b

The woman in this poem does something slightly scandalous. She gets up in the middle of the night, goes out into the city streets alone, stops the watchmen to ask if they've seen the one she loves. It's urgent, undignified, not the behavior of someone who has composed herself. She doesn't find him by being patient or pious. She finds him almost accidentally — I had hardly left them — the moment she stops interrogating the watchmen and moves on.

The Song of Songs is a love poem, erotic and direct, and there has always been a tradition of reading it as something more: the soul searching for God, or the Church for Christ, desire itself as a kind of theology. Whether or not you take that reading, the texture of the poem is worth sitting with today. The searching is real. The not-finding is real. The city at night, the watchmen who can't help, the sense that the beloved is nowhere — all of that is in the poem and none of it is resolved by faith or prayer. She finds him because she keeps moving.

The Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18

Mary comes to the tomb while it is still dark. That detail matters. She is not arriving in the clear light of morning with her grief organized; she comes in the dark, and the darkness is both literal and otherwise. She finds the stone moved, runs to tell Peter and the beloved disciple, and then — when the two men have gone home, satisfied with the evidence — she stays.

That staying is worth a long look. She doesn't go home. She weeps outside a tomb she already knows is empty. When the angels ask why she is weeping, she tells them the same thing she told Peter: she doesn't know where they put him. Then she turns and finds a man she takes for the gardener, and she asks him the same question.

She is so deep in her grief and her searching that she cannot recognize what she is looking at. This is not a failure of faith. It is what grief does. And then he says her name — just that, Mary — and she knows him immediately.

The commission he gives her is stark. Stop holding on, he says, and go tell the others. She has just found him after three days of loss, and the first thing she is asked to do is let go again, at least of the form she has been looking for. She goes. I have seen the Lord. That is the whole message. The tradition has called her apostle to the apostles ever since, the first to carry the news, sent precisely because she would not leave.

Where the two readings meet

The Song of Songs and John 20 are not on separate tracks today. They are, almost eerily, the same scene. A woman searches at night for the one she loves, cannot find him, asks everyone she meets, and then — barely past the last person who couldn't help — finds him. The poem could be Mary Magdalene's interior life on Holy Saturday and into the dark of Easter morning.

What the Gospel adds is the moment of recognition, and what causes it. It isn't the empty tomb. It isn't the angels. It isn't even the conversation with a stranger. It's her name, spoken by the right voice. And what the Song of Songs adds to the Gospel is the rawness of the searching — no calm trust, no patient waiting, just going out into the city at night asking strangers.

Together they say something about what love looks like before it finds what it's looking for. It looks a little desperate. It is not always dignified. And it turns out that is not a problem.

To carry today

What form of him are you holding on to so tightly that you might be missing the one already standing in front of you — the living one, not the one laid in the place you keep looking?
