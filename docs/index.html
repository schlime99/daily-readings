---

The Feast and Its Saint

Mary Magdalene holds a singular place in the Gospel story. She is the one who stood at the cross when others fled, the one who came to the tomb before dawn, and the one to whom the Risen Lord first appeared and entrusted the announcement of resurrection. Pope Francis elevated her memorial to the rank of feast in 2016, a recognition that she is, in the ancient phrase of the tradition, *apostola apostolorum* — the apostle to the apostles. Today's readings are chosen with great care to honor that identity.

---

The First Reading: A Heart That Will Not Stop Searching

The Song of Songs is a love poem — extravagant, earthy, and deeply personal. In its literary setting, a bride searches frantically through the nighttime city for her beloved. She asks the city watchmen; she presses on without waiting for their answer; and then, almost before she can register it, she finds him. The searching is urgent, almost desperate: the phrase *him whom my heart loves* repeats like a heartbeat that cannot slow down.

The Church has long read the Song of Songs on two levels at once: as a celebration of human love in its proper glory, and as an image of the soul's longing for God. The bride searching in the dark streets becomes every person who has ever felt the absence of the divine and refused to make peace with that absence.

---

The Gospel: The Garden Before Dawn

John's resurrection account places Mary Magdalene at the tomb while it is still dark — a detail that is both historical and symbolic. She comes in grief and finds the stone rolled away. She runs for Peter and the beloved disciple, who come, see, and go home. But Mary stays. She cannot leave. She weeps, she bends to look again, she speaks to angels, and then she mistakes the Risen Jesus for a gardener — until he says her name. That single word, *Mary*, is the turning point of the entire passage. She recognizes him not by sight but by voice, and she responds with everything she has: *Rabbouni* — my Teacher, my Lord.

Jesus then sends her: go and tell my brothers. The woman who could not stop searching becomes the first witness and the first herald of the resurrection.

---

The Thread Between Them

The connection here is not forced — it is exact. The bride of the Song goes out searching in the dark and finds her beloved. Mary goes to the tomb in the dark and finds the Risen Lord. Both moments are defined by the same relentless, love-driven seeking. Both end in sudden, almost overwhelming recognition and encounter.

There is one poignant difference. The bride of the Song seizes her beloved and will not let him go. Jesus tells Mary, *Stop holding on to me* — not a rejection, but a redirection. The love that found him must now move outward. Encounter becomes mission. Mary cannot cling to the moment; she is commissioned to carry it.

Psalm 63 holds both readings together perfectly: *my soul thirsts for you like parched earth without water*. That thirst is the Song's searching bride, and it is Mary weeping in the garden. The psalm then pivots to satisfaction and praise — the soul that clings to God is upheld. Mary's story makes that pivot in real time, right before our eyes.

The Optional Second Reading

If 2 Corinthians 5 is used, Paul adds a further layer: because Christ died and rose, the old way of knowing anyone — including Christ himself — is finished. Mary experienced exactly this. She had known Jesus *according to the flesh*, as teacher, companion, Lord. The resurrection requires her to know him in a new way, and to carry that new knowledge to others. Paul's *behold, new things have come* is Mary's *I have seen the Lord* in theological shorthand.

---

To carry today

Where in your own life are you still searching in the dark — and are you willing to stay at the tomb long enough to hear your name called?
