She is just moving, because staying still is worse.\n\nThe Song of Songs is a love poem, probably a collection of them, and the rabbis argued for a long time about whether it belonged in the canon at all. It won its place partly because the tradition read it as more than erotic verse — as something that maps the shape of the soul's longing for God. That reading has stayed alive in the Church, not because the literal meaning gets discarded but because desire turned toward God actually feels like this: the empty bed, the dark city, the slightly embarrassing urgency of asking anyone who might know where he has gone.\n\nThe bride finds him almost by accident — "I had hardly left them when I found him." Just: she was searching and then she wasn't anymore.\n\nThe Gospel — John 20:1-2, 11-18\n\nJohn's account of the empty tomb opens with a detail that the other Gospels don't linger on: it is still dark. Mary comes before sunrise, in the in-between hour, and finds the stone moved and the body gone. She runs to tell the disciples. Then, after Peter and the other disciple have come and gone, she stays.\n\nThat staying is worth sitting with. The disciples look and leave. Mary weeps and does not move. And it is while she is weeping — not in a moment of faith or clarity — that she bends into the tomb and sees the angels, and then turns and sees a man she takes for the gardener. The recognition doesn't come because she has composed herself. It comes when he says her name.\n\nOne word: Mary. And she turns — the Greek suggests a full, sudden turn, the body catching up to something the heart already knew. She calls him Rabbouni, Teacher, and she holds on. And then he gives her a task before she can catch her breath: go tell the brothers.\n\nThe tradition has called her the apostle to the apostles, the first to carry the resurrection news, because that is simply what happened. She went and told them: I have seen the Lord.\n\nThe search and the finding\n\nThe Song of Songs and the Gospel are not on separate tracks today — the Feast exists precisely because the Church heard Mary's story and reached for this poem. The fit is almost uncomfortably close. Both women search in the dark. Both ask where the beloved has been taken. Both find him not through strategy but through sudden grace.\n\nBut there is a difference the Gospel adds that the poem doesn't quite reach. In Song of Songs, the bride finds and holds. In the Gospel, Mary finds and is told — gently but clearly — not to hold. Stop clinging to me. He is not going back to what they were before. The resurrection is not a restoration of the old arrangements. Something new has come, and she has to go tell it rather than stay and keep it.\n\nThat is a harder ending than the poem gives us. The joy is real. The finding is real. And then you are sent.\n\nThe psalm — Psalm 63:2,3-4,5-6,8-9\n\nThe psalm holds all of this in one image: a parched land, and a soul that thirsts the same way. My soul clings fast to you — the same clinging Mary is told to release, and yet here it is named as the right posture of the soul. Perhaps what Mary is asked to release is not the love but the particular form it was taking: the wish to have the old Jesus back, on the old terms, in the old garden.\n\nTo carry today\n\nMary recognized him only when he said her name — not when she was looking directly at him.
